The Pattern

Development gets approved. The infrastructure never follows.

It's the same story across Clermont County: large developments are approved on roads that were already near capacity, with no plan to expand the roads to match. By the time traffic arrives, the fixes are years away — if they come at all. SR 28 is the clearest, best-documented example, but it isn't the only corridor under this pressure, and CART's scope is county-wide.

CART isn't anti-development. We believe growth that's planned and infrastructure-ready is good for our communities. The problem is the order things happen in — approval first, roads maybe later — and the way each project is judged in isolation while the corridor as a whole quietly fills past its limit.

The SR 28 Story · 2013–2026

One study. A clear warning. Thirteen years of inaction.

In 2013, Clermont County paid for the only comprehensive technical study SR 28 has ever had. It said exactly what needed to happen. Here's what it found, what it promised — and what was actually built.

What it found

2013 Corridor Study
  • SR 28 already at 91% of capacity at the western end during evening peak.
  • Gridlock (10–15 mph average speeds) projected by 2030 without major improvements.
  • 173 access points along 4+ miles — far beyond ODOT's standards.

What it promised

The study's own plan
  • Widen SR 28 to four lanes with turn lanes at major intersections.
  • Rebuild and consolidate the Branch Hill Guinea Pike intersection.
  • Improvements would be triggered by development — roads improve as growth arrives.

What was built

Thirteen years later
  • SR 28 widened to four lanes — never done.
  • Branch Hill Guinea Pike rebuilt — never done.
  • One turn lane added to 2,400 ft of Branch Hill Guinea Pike — the entire physical legacy of the study.
The study's plan said the road would improve as development came in. The exact opposite happened — the development came, and the improvements didn't. We're now four years from the gridlock the study warned about, with far more growth on the way than its projections ever assumed.

What We're Asking For

A new, funded corridor study — the foundation for everything else.

We're not asking the state to commit to widening SR 28 tomorrow. We're asking for the current technical study that makes funded improvements possible. The 2013 study gave us the diagnosis. A 2026 study gives us the treatment plan.

New study → State funding program → Funded improvements → SR 28 widened & intersection rebuilt
  • Assess the cumulative impact of all the new development — not one project at a time in isolation.
  • Prioritize the SR 28 / Branch Hill Guinea Pike intersection, now flanked by two new developments.
  • Coordinate with Goshen Township, Miami Township, and the County Engineer.
  • Tie the findings to the state's funding program so improvements have a real path forward.
